About This List
Why it exists and how it works
My sister asked me to suggest something to watch. Instead, I built a guide. Turns out that was the easier option.

Not a quick list. Not a top ten. A curated, organized, searchable collection of everything I actually watch and love — built so that anyone who knows me can open it and find something great without having to ask.

Everything here is something I've personally seen. Maybe a couple of exceptions. But close to all of it.

I've been watching film seriously for a long time. I have opinions. I have taste. And I have range — from Spike Lee to Jet Li, from Sade to Saul Williams, from Before Sunrise to Ip Man. This list reflects all of it.

PCM Personality Types
Process Communication Model
PCM decodes human interaction by identifying how individuals perceive the world, communicate, motivate themselves, and behave under stress. Regarding living, receiving life, and expressing oneself, PCM posits that we all possess a unique blend of six personality types — and that living effectively means understanding your own blend of personalities and adapting your communication to connect authentically with others.

What does that have to do with watching a movie? More than you'd think. The stories that move us, the characters we root for, the themes that stick — these aren't random. They tend to reflect how we already process the world. A Persister gravitates toward moral weight. A Rebel wants energy and surprise. A Harmonizer needs warmth — stories where the relationships are real and the love between people actually means something. Knowing your type — or even just your top two — is a surprisingly reliable shortcut to finding something you'll actually connect with. Use the PCM filters on the main page to explore.

Want to find your type? Open the assistant and tap "Find my PCM type" — Lil Steve will ask if you already know your stack or walk you through a short quiz to find out. Already know your type? Tap "I know my PCM type" and go straight to recommendations.
💎 Persister
Driven by values, beliefs, and integrity. Wants stories where the moral stakes are real and the characters have to choose. At their best: principled, dedicated, observant. Under stress: preaches, moralizes, becomes rigid.
Films that tend to land: Glory, Malcolm X, 12 Years a Slave, Ip Man, Creed, The Pursuit of Happyness
🎨 Imaginer
Reflective, inner-directed, drawn to the unique and the beautiful. Needs space to contemplate. At their best: imaginative, calm, thoughtful. Under stress: withdraws, becomes passive, loses initiative.
Films that tend to land: Lost in Translation, Her, Medicine for Melancholy, Beasts of the Southern Wild, Eyes Wide Shut
⚙️ Thinker
Logical, organized, loves a well-structured narrative with a satisfying payoff. Appreciates cleverness and competence. At their best: precise, analytical, responsible. Under stress: over-controls, becomes perfectionistic.
Films that tend to land: Inception, The Prestige, Primer, Memento, Inside Man, The Game
🌸 Harmonizer
Warm, empathetic, relationship-driven. Needs warmth — stories where the relationships are real and the love between people actually means something. At their best: compassionate, sensitive, caring. Under stress: makes mistakes, becomes over-accommodating.
Films that tend to land: Love Jones, Soul Food, The Best Man, Before Sunrise, Waiting to Exhale, Brown Sugar
⚡ Rebel
Spontaneous, playful, drawn to energy, humor, and creative irreverence. Wants to be entertained and surprised. At their best: creative, funny, energetic. Under stress: complains, becomes negative, stirs things up.
Films that tend to land: Coming to America, Friday, Office Space, Scott Pilgrim, White Chicks, Hot Tub Time Machine
🚀 Promoter
Action-oriented, charming, drawn to high stakes, power, and results. Wants momentum and doesn't have patience for slow burns. At their best: resourceful, adaptable, persuasive. Under stress: manipulates, becomes exploitative.
Films that tend to land: Training Day, John Wick, Bad Boys, Scarface, Power, Snowfall

Genre Definitions
What each genre is really about
💥 Action & Thriller
Physical conflict escalating toward a decisive confrontation. The hero must act — not just think — and the moral question is usually about what they are willing to do and what line they won't cross. The best action films use spectacle to reveal character.
🎤 Biographical
The hardest question a narrative can ask: who does a person truly become? The biographical film traces a real life to find the moment of transformation — the decision, the price, the legacy. The best ones refuse to flatten their subject into myth.
🎞️ Classic Cinema & Noir
Hollywood's golden era revealed a world of moral ambiguity dressed in gorgeous black and white. Noir especially treats desire as danger and the city as a trap. These films reward patience and attention — their pleasures are craft, atmosphere, and the weight of fate.
😄 Comedy
Comedy is not simply jokes — it is a surgical exposé of social hypocrisy. The comic hero is uniquely blind to their own flaw, stumbling through a world that reveals the gap between who we pretend to be and who we truly are. Great comedy results in genuine self-recognition.
🔍 Crime
Crime stories explore society's moral underside. Every crime narrative asks: who is truly corrupt? The best crime dramas use the law as a mirror, revealing that the line between criminal and citizen is thinner than we admit.
🎥 Documentary & Stand-Up
Documentary storytelling follows the same structure as fiction: a subject with a problem, an investigation, a revelation. The best documentaries change what you know about the world. Stand-up comedy is documentary of a different kind — one person's unfiltered truth.
🎭 Drama
Drama lives in the space between who a character believes they are and who they truly are. The opponent is often internal — fear, pride, grief, denial. Drama resolves not through action but through revelation: a character finally sees themselves clearly.
🦸 Fantasy & Superhero
Fantasy uses the impossible to examine what is most human. Superhero stories are mythology for the modern age — the hero's extraordinary power is matched by an extraordinary burden. The real story is always about identity, responsibility, and the cost of being different.
👁️ Horror & Suspense
Horror confronts what civilization cannot contain. The monster — whether creature, institution, or idea — threatens not just life but the soul. The real horror is always psychological: the monster shows us what we fear most about ourselves.
🥋 Martial Arts
Martial arts stories use the body as the instrument of philosophical inquiry. Physical mastery is never the end — it is the path. Every battle tests not just skill but character, discipline, and wisdom. The true fight is always the conquest of one's own ego and limitation.
🎵 Musical
Musicals solve one of storytelling's oldest problems: how do you show what a character feels inside? Song and dance make the invisible visible — emotion so large it cannot fit in ordinary speech. The musical is inherently optimistic, insisting that human feeling is worth celebrating.
❤️ Romance
Romance is not about meeting — it is about two people who both want love and are both preventing themselves from having it. The story resolves only when both are willing to be fully vulnerable. True romance is always about two people becoming more fully themselves.
🚀 Science Fiction
Science fiction asks "what if" — and uses the answer to examine the present. The best sci-fi is never about robots or aliens; it is about consciousness, identity, freedom, and the consequences of human ambition. The imagination is the most important instrument of moral inquiry we have.
🏆 Sports
The physical contest is always a proxy for the hero's inner battle — with their own fear, their past, their limits. Winning or losing is almost beside the point. What matters is who the hero becomes in the process of competing. The body under pressure reveals the truth.
⚔️ War & Historical
War and historical stories use the specificity of time and conflict to examine universal moral questions about sacrifice, power, and human dignity. The best war narratives ask what a person owes to others, and what they are willing to endure. History is never finished with us.
